All the data from this page was collected from this database of finishing statistics for the Toronto Waterfront Marathon. I omitted all participants who did not finish or were disqualified.

Finishing Time

The plot below shows the finishing times of participants of the Toronto Waterfront Marathon from 2002 to 2016. You can filter the results by age, year, and gender.

You can really see the big bump below the 3:00 mark, the 3:30 mark, and the 4:00 mark. These are goal times for many people, and large groups that are following pacers will appear as bumps here. It's interesting to see that the median pace hasn't changed much over the years. I was expecting that as the popularity of the marathon grew, that the overall pace would decrease, but if this trend exists, it isn't very obvious in the data. I ran the 2015 and 2016 marathons, and I was happy to see that everyone else agreed that 2016 was the more difficult year -- in fact, it was the slowest race (by median finish time) of the last fifteen years!

Splits

The plot below compares the pace of the runners during different segments of the race to their average pace over the entire race. For example, the violin plot labelled '0-10km' measures the (pace for first 10k, (sec/km)) minus (entire race pace (sec/km)). The big bump around -8 means that a lot of people ran their first 10k at a pace roughly -8 seconds/km faster than their overall race pace. You can filter these results by age, gender, and finishing time. You can also change which segment you're comparing against by clicking on the title of the plot.
It's fun to watch how the faster runners tend to have much more even splits, but still tend to start out fast and then slow down. I was surprised by how much -- I'm going to have to track down that sub-2:30 runner who did their 35-40km split a minute slower than their race pace, and see what happened!
Also, it's amazing how well the 21.1-30km pace predicts your overall race pace. So if you're at 30km, maybe the best way to predict your result is to . At least, this gives you some mental math to keep your mind off the pain of the last 12k of the race. A few warnings about this visualization:
  • The distance in the segment 40-42.2km is much shorter than the other distances. Because of this, for purely statistical reasons we'd expect there to be more variability in runners' paces over 40-42.2km than in the other segments -- even ignoring the fact that there are other psychological and physiological reasons people go abnormally fast or slow at the end of a marathon. So don't overanalyze the 40-42.2km segment, it's just there for fun!
  • There are problems with the 2012 data -- many runners are recorded as finishing the race before they hit the 40km checkpoint. I think this is an issue with the 40km checkpoint, so the plot above does not include the data from 2012 when visualizing any statistic that uses the 35-40km or 40km-42.2km paces.
